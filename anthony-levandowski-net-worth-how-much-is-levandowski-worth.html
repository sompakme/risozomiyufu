Anthony Levandowski: Otto and Pronto

After leaving Google, Anthony Levandowski co-founded Otto, an autonomous trucking company, with Lior Ron, Don Burnette, and Claire Delaunay. Otto quickly gained traction and was acquired by Uber Technologies within just five months of its launch. Levandowski then became the leader of Uber’s driverless car division. He later launched another company called Pronto, which initially focused on retrofitting semi-trucks with self-driving systems. Levandowski’s ventures in the autonomous trucking industry demonstrated his continued involvement in cutting-edge technology.

The Rise of Otto

When Levandowski left Google, he decided to focus his expertise on the autonomous trucking industry. In 2016, he co-founded Otto, a company dedicated to developing self-driving technology for long-haul trucks. Leveraging his experience and knowledge from the Google self-driving car program, Levandowski and his team successfully retrofitted existing trucks with autonomous systems. The rapid growth and potential of Otto caught the attention of Uber, leading to its acquisition for a reported $680 million. This acquisition not only solidified Levandowski’s position as a leading figure in the self-driving industry but also provided him with the resources and support of a global transportation network.

Pronto: Pioneering Self-Driving Semi-Trucks

Following the success of Otto, Levandowski founded Pronto, another autonomous trucking company. Pronto aimed to further advance and refine self-driving technology for semi-trucks. The company focused on retrofitting existing trucks with advanced sensors, artificial intelligence systems, and autonomous driving capabilities. By retrofitting rather than manufacturing new vehicles, Pronto aimed to expedite the adoption of self-driving technology in the trucking industry. Levandowski’s vision for Pronto was to revolutionize the logistics and transportation sector by making it safer, more efficient, and environmentally friendly.

Civil Lawsuit and Federal Conviction

In 2017, Anthony Levandowski found himself at the center of a high-profile civil lawsuit between Waymo (formerly Google’s self-driving car project) and Uber. Waymo accused Levandowski of stealing trade secrets and using them at Uber. The lawsuit, which captivated the tech industry, resulted in a settlement wherein Uber agreed to pay Waymo approximately $245 million in equity and pledged not to utilize Waymo’s technology in their self-driving vehicles.

Waymo accused Levandowski of stealing trade secrets and using them at Uber.

The fallout from the civil lawsuit didn’t end there for Levandowski. He also faced federal charges for theft of trade secrets, which eventually led to a conviction and an 18-month prison sentence. Levandowski served approximately six months of his sentence before being granted a pardon by President Donald Trump.
